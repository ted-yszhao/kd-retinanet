\chapter{Conclusions}

This dissertation set out to study whether knowledge distillation can improve an efficient one-stage object detector without increasing inference-time cost \cite{hinton2015distilling}. The proposed answer was a RetinaNet teacher-student framework in which a stronger frozen detector supervises a smaller student through an SSIM-based structural loss applied to aligned FPN features \cite{lin2017fpn,lin2017focal,wang2004ssim,derijk2022structural}. Across this report, that idea was developed from first principles, connected to the relevant literature, implemented in a configuration-driven training system, and tested through multiple COCO-style experiments under a realistic single-GPU budget \cite{lin2014coco}.

\section{Achievement Against Objectives}

Against the objectives stated in Chapter 1, several important outcomes have been achieved. First, a practical RetinaNet distillation framework is defined with interchangeable teacher and student backbones, creating a controlled basis for comparison within a single detector family. Second, SSIM-based structural distillation is adapted to level-aligned FPN representations and the discussion justifies why this interface is more suitable for the task than direct raw-backbone matching. Third, a dimensional analysis of the teacher and student feature spaces is provided, showing why no additional learned adaptation layer is required after the FPN. Fourth, the full optimization and evaluation pipeline is implemented with configuration control, checkpointing, and COCO-style metrics.

Most importantly, the dissertation now reports real quantitative evidence rather than only a proposed framework. Under the implemented budget-constrained training regime, a vanilla \texttt{ResNet-18} RetinaNet reaches $0.1515$ mAP on the COCO validation set, while a \texttt{ResNet-101} teacher distilled into the same \texttt{ResNet-18} student reaches $0.1636$ mAP. A student-only \texttt{ResNet-50} baseline reaches $0.1830$ mAP, and a \texttt{ResNet-101} teacher distilled into the same \texttt{ResNet-50} student improves that result to $0.1954$ mAP. The distilled students therefore improve AP not only in the compact target case but also in the larger-student control case. Because the final checkpoint remains the same student model in each setting, these gains come without increasing the deployed model size. The compact \texttt{ResNet-18} result remains the central positive finding of the study, and it supports the claim that post-FPN structural supervision can help one-stage detectors recover some of the representational strength of deeper teachers.

The experiments also show that the method is not automatically stable for every teacher-student pairing. A \texttt{ResNet-50} teacher distilled into a \texttt{ResNet-18} student collapses to zero AP and exhibits extreme loss instability. Rather than being omitted, this result is treated as part of the contribution. It demonstrates that detector distillation is sensitive to optimization details and that the success of a structural KD objective depends on how teacher capacity, student capacity, and hyperparameters interact. As a result, the dissertation contributes both a promising success case and a concrete diagnostic warning for future work.

\section{Future Work}

The most immediate extension is a more systematic ablation study. The current experiments use one KD weight, one SSIM window size, and a single selected internal feature key. Future work should vary the pyramid levels, introduce level-specific weights, and test whether a curriculum or warm-up schedule for $\lambda$ stabilizes difficult teacher-student pairings. This is especially important in light of the failed \texttt{ResNet-50} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{ResNet-18} run, which suggests that optimization stability may depend strongly on how and when structural supervision is introduced.

It would also be valuable to expand the set of baselines further. Now that a student-only \texttt{ResNet-50} run is available, the next useful controls would be repeated seeds, a student-only \texttt{ResNet-101} reference under the same shortened schedule, and matched comparisons against simple $\ell_1$ or $\ell_2$ feature losses. Additional tests against more detector-specific KD strategies such as foreground-guided or relation-based methods would make it possible to determine whether SSIM is the best structural signal for this setting or simply one strong option among several.

Beyond these immediate extensions, the work opens a broader line of research on efficiency-oriented detector transfer. The present method uses only one-stage RetinaNet models and closed-set COCO supervision, but the same principles could be tested with stronger backbones, longer schedules, or transformer-based detectors when compute permits. The central lesson is that structural feature transfer is promising, but it must be treated as an optimization problem as well as a representation problem. Future work that combines the current post-FPN interface with stronger training stabilization and richer multi-level supervision is therefore likely to be the most productive next step.

\section{Critical Appraisal}

Taken as a whole, this project makes a modest but concrete contribution to efficient object detection research. It does not claim to introduce a fully optimized state-of-the-art detector, nor does it attempt to cover the full modern design space of distillation, transformer detection, or multimodal grounding. Instead, it focuses on a narrower practical question: within a standard RetinaNet framework, can structural feature supervision help a compact detector under the kinds of resource constraints that many projects actually face? The experiments suggest that it can, provided that the teacher-student pairing and optimization remain well behaved.

This framing matters academically because it clarifies what kind of contribution is being made. Some dissertations are strongest as new theory, some as large empirical benchmarks, and others as carefully designed systems studies. The present work sits closest to the third category. Its value comes from combining a literature-grounded method, a clear implementation, and honest interpretation of the resulting experiments. The post-FPN SSIM-based KD loss is not presented as a black-box trick; instead, the report explains why the FPN interface is semantically meaningful, why channel alignment removes the need for an adaptation layer, how the loss is computed in practice, and how the resulting system behaves on real COCO training traces.

The negative result is part of that contribution. In research on model compression and distillation, there is a natural temptation to treat unstable runs as mere engineering accidents and leave them out of the final narrative. That would be a mistake here. The zero-AP \texttt{ResNet-50} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{ResNet-18} run shows that structural detector distillation is not universally robust under fixed hyperparameters. Recording that fact gives the work more scientific value, not less, because it helps future work focus on the conditions under which structural supervision is beneficial or hazardous. In a viva or final assessment, that kind of honest boundary-setting is more persuasive than over-claiming.

There is also a broader practical lesson. Real deployment work is frequently constrained not by theoretical interest, but by memory budgets, training budgets, and timelines. In those conditions, a method that gives a compact detector a measurable AP gain while keeping the same deployed checkpoint can be more useful than a more sophisticated approach that requires far greater retraining cost or infrastructure complexity. The project therefore contributes a small but realistic example of how careful architectural alignment and feature-level supervision can create value even when the experimental budget is far from ideal. That emphasis on practical, reproducible efficiency is ultimately the perspective that unifies the report.
